# My Open Questions

The twelve questions I'm sitting with right now. Feynman–Rota practice: keep a dozen favorite problems live, and test every new input against them. Every once in a while there's a hit.


---

## 1. What patterns emerge as AI markets experiment with outcome-pricing instead of tokens?

AI is priced by the token today — by the raw unit of computation. That turns "is this worth it?" into a continuous cost calculation rather than a question about value delivered. Outcome-pricing experiments are starting to appear: agentic services, vertical AI products, expertise-bundling startups. The question is what these experiments actually produce in practice — where outcome-pricing holds up, where it collapses back to tokens, and what trust and measurement primitives turn out to matter in real markets rather than thought experiments.

## 2. What happens to the world economy if personal-scale nuclear displaces oil?

A Feynman-style favorite problem — held with curiosity rather than active research. If our primary energy source becomes distributed and small-scale rather than concentrated and fossil, almost everything shifts: geopolitics, labor, transport, trade, and the geography of where power (in both senses) is held. The question is what that world actually looks like.

## 3. What patterns emerge in how self-evolving products choose between thesis and users?

A product has a thesis (what it's trying to be) and a usage pattern (how people are actually using it). The two pull in different directions, and leaning entirely on either has known failure modes — drifting away from reality, or getting stuck only serving the users it already has. When the product is evolving itself, how does it choose, and where does either end of the spectrum stop being progress and start being decay?

## 4. What shifts when a person's learned AI travels with them across roles?

If an agent is an extension of a person's thinking — trained on their taste, their work, their patterns — what happens when they change employers and the agent comes with them? The interesting work is on the labor side: how career shape, the worker–firm power balance, and the contours of employment shift when the AI a person works with belongs to them, not the company.

## 5. How will AI be used for acts of radical creativity?

Not "can AI be creative?" — that question is well-trodden. The real one: when AI trained on prior creative work is in the loop, what does work that's genuinely *new* actually look like? An area held with curiosity rather than active practice. The inquiry is into the specific practices, workflows, and prompting disciplines that reliably push past pastiche and produce moves nobody has made before.

## 6. How do we build modern-day salons that become engines of invention in an increasingly polarized world?

The Lunar Society of Birmingham (1765–1813) — Boulton, Watt, Priestley, Erasmus Darwin, Wedgwood — set the conditions for the Industrial Revolution by mixing hands-on making with scientific inquiry across class, religious, and political lines. That kind of collective output (Brian Eno's "scenius") radically exceeds what any individual could do alone. Personal stake: I want to convene one and don't yet know what it takes. The question is the specific practices, conveners, and conditions that have actually produced scenius — and what it would take now, when convening across those lines is harder than it's ever been.

## 7. How do you keep a team grounded when the pace of change never lets up?

Constant change is the steady state, and the cynical read — adjust or burn out — is cheap and available. The real question is what a leader actively gives a team in that environment so people stay oriented rather than numb. A practice rather than a posture: what to hold constant, what to let move, and how to make the pace itself feel navigable.

## 8. How do you avoid team misalignment with a constitutional roadmap as production accelerates?

When shipping speed collapses the durability of a six- or twelve-month plan, the roadmap stops doing its alignment job — the plan is stale before the team gets there. What replaces it? A continuous plan, layered horizons, something else entirely? And how do you hold humans together against a target that won't sit still, without either over-constraining them or letting them drift?

## 9. How can we make contemporary housing as exciting and economical as mid-century modern was to build?

Mid-century modern is widely loved and was inexpensive to build. Today's housing — especially affordable housing — is aesthetically dire, and the binding constraint isn't aesthetics or construction cost. Something else is in the way: zoning, finance, developer incentives, risk pricing, labor supply. The live question is finding the actual blocker and designing around it, so the architecture of the mid-century could happen again rather than stay a nostalgic reference point.

## 10. How do you design a thinking system that travels with you across tools, jobs, and years?

Personal knowledge tools come and go; employers come and go; the way a person thinks needs to be its own durable artifact rather than a by-product of whatever stack happens to be in use. This wiki you're reading is itself an attempt at the answer. The question is what principles produce a thinking system with that kind of longevity — what's structural, what's transferable, and what quietly depends on tools that won't be here in two years.

## 11. What about product creation practice should we fight to preserve as AI takes over the work?

AI is rewriting how products actually get made — design critique, user research, code review, mentorship, the slow accumulation of judgment that comes from working with real users and real systems. Some of that is overhead worth shedding; some of it is the actual education that produces a thoughtful designer, PM, or engineer. Personal stake: twenty years inside the practice means I'm watching it being rewritten in real time. The question is which parts the new tooling will retire before anyone notices they were the load-bearing ones.

## 12. What does the AI moment ask of someone with my experience?

Twenty-plus years of shipping software, leading teams, and navigating regulated environments are real assets. The AI moment is reshaping all of that, and the usual framing ("what can I contribute?") slides too easily into savior-mode. Inverting the agency: what is this moment actually demanding of someone with compounding experience, and where — specifically, not heroically — is the slice where that experience bites hardest?
